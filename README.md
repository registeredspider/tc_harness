Executive Feedback – Spoken Guide
1. Opening / Introduction

“Thank you for taking the time. I’ve spent close to 3.9 years here, and I’ve always given my best—whether it was delivering projects, supporting the team, or ensuring continuity during critical situations. I’d like to share some constructive feedback from my experience.”

2. Process & Approvals

“There were instances where established processes didn’t feel consistent. For example, approved leave was questioned after the fact, which created unnecessary confusion. Situations like this can make team members hesitant to request planned leave in advance. In my 17.5 years across major companies, I have not faced this type of situation before.”

3. Clarity & Alignment

“There were gaps in clarity regarding expectations and ownership of tasks. For instance, during EQLX estimation, code coverage was below 10% while the target expectation was around 60%.
When discussing the tester’s role, confusion arose between unit testing and automation, which highlighted a gap in understanding responsibilities.
Additionally, when I returned from PTO, I wasn’t fully briefed on task status, yet accountability expectations remained.”

4. Knowledge Transfer & SQL MI Support

“Knowledge transfer sessions were not fully supported; some team members joined in ways that hindered participation, such as via phone without screen sharing.
SQL MI work, which started 2–2.5 years ago, lacked sufficient documentation. The last person handling the work had limited knowledge, as they were primarily involved in testing.
I did not receive much initial support from the India-side manager and had to rely on my own network to progress the work, including late-night coordination across India and the US.
The US-side manager, however, was highly engaged and provided necessary guidance, which helped move things forward.

Additionally, during critical evening situations, support from the India team was limited. Some members joined calls via phone or did not participate fully. When I raised this, the focus seemed more on encouraging early arrivals and leaving early rather than ensuring effective overlap. I personally ensured maximum US-team overlap, but systemic support from India was lacking.”

5. Leadership Engagement

“During critical incident calls, engagement levels varied. In some cases, the India-side manager provided limited input, whereas the US-side manager consistently stayed engaged, provided direction, and helped resolve issues effectively.
This difference in engagement clearly impacts team coordination during high-pressure situations.”

6. Professional Environment

“There were also a few comments that didn’t feel aligned with an inclusive workplace. Despite this, I remained committed—balancing family responsibilities, supporting weekend work, and extending calls to maintain US-team overlap without requesting compensatory time off.”

7. Ownership & Delivery

“Despite these challenges, I ensured delivery, extended support where needed, and maintained continuity for the team.”

8. Summary / Recommendation

“My goal is not to assign blame but to highlight patterns that, if improved, could significantly enhance team effectiveness, clarity, and the overall experience for current and future team members.
I appreciate the opportunity I had here and hope this feedback is useful.”

✅ Tips for Speaking

Think of the headings as mental cues rather than reading them aloud.
Pause after key points: SQL MI support, evening overlap, incident engagement.
Maintain a calm, factual tone—focus on impact and observations, not personalities.
Speak slowly, emphasize critical points, and finish confidently with the summary.
